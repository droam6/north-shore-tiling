Kitchen Splashback Trends for 2026: What's Popular in Sydney

February 5, 2026 • 8 min read

The kitchen splashback has evolved well beyond its practical origins as a wall protector behind the stove. In 2026, it is one of the most important design statements in any Sydney kitchen renovation. Whether you are updating a Federation home in Mosman or renovating a modern apartment kitchen in Chatswood, the splashback you choose sets the visual tone for the entire room. Here is our guide to the materials, styles and colour trends that are shaping Sydney kitchens this year.

Subway Tiles: Still Popular, But Evolving

The subway tile is not going anywhere in 2026, but the way homeowners are using it has changed significantly. The classic 75x150mm white subway tile in a horizontal brick pattern, while still timeless, is no longer the default choice for every kitchen. Instead, we are seeing a clear shift toward larger format subway tiles, particularly the 100x300mm and 100x400mm sizes that offer a more contemporary, elongated look.

Vertical stacking layouts have become particularly popular with Sydney designers this year. Running subway tiles vertically rather than in the traditional offset pattern creates a fresh, modern feel that draws the eye upward and makes ceilings appear taller. This layout works especially well in galley kitchens, which are common in North Shore apartments and terraces.

Coloured grout is another way homeowners are breathing new life into the subway tile format. A charcoal or black grout line paired with white tiles creates a bold graphic effect, while a matching tone-on-tone grout gives a softer, more seamless appearance. We are also seeing growing interest in handmade or zellige-style subway tiles, which have slightly irregular edges and a glossy, undulating surface that catches light beautifully.

Terrazzo-Look Tiles: A Major Comeback

Terrazzo has made a dramatic return to Australian interiors, and the kitchen splashback is one of the most popular places to use it. Rather than traditional poured terrazzo, most homeowners are opting for terrazzo-look porcelain tiles that replicate the speckled aggregate pattern at a fraction of the cost and maintenance burden. These tiles come in a range of chip sizes and background colours, from soft white with pastel flecks through to warm grey with bolder terracotta and sage inclusions. Terrazzo-look tiles pair beautifully with solid-colour benchtops and add visual texture without overwhelming the space.

Natural Stone Splashbacks: Marble and Travertine

For homeowners looking to make a premium statement, natural stone splashbacks remain one of the most sought-after options in 2026. Calacatta and Carrara marble continue to dominate the luxury end of the market, with their distinctive veining creating a splashback that doubles as a piece of art. The trend this year is to run the same stone from the benchtop up the wall, creating a seamless, monolithic look that is particularly striking with bookmatched slabs.

Travertine is the natural stone surprise of 2026. Once associated with Tuscan-style interiors from the early 2000s, it has been reinterpreted for contemporary kitchens with honed and filled finishes in warm cream and walnut tones. Its naturally varied surface adds organic warmth and works exceptionally well in kitchens that feature timber cabinetry and brass or brushed gold hardware.

Patterned and Moroccan-Style Tiles

Patterned tiles are having a strong moment in Sydney kitchen design. Moroccan-inspired encaustic-look tiles, featuring geometric patterns in earthy tones, add character and personality to a kitchen without requiring a full renovation. These tiles are particularly effective as a feature splashback behind the cooktop, framed by simpler tiles or painted walls on either side. In 2026, the patterns are becoming more refined and subtle, moving away from the bold, high-contrast patterns of a few years ago toward softer, tonal designs that blend with the surrounding cabinetry.

Large Format Porcelain Panels: The Minimal Grout Solution

Large format porcelain panels are one of the fastest-growing trends in the Sydney splashback market. Available in sizes up to 1200x2400mm or even larger, these engineered slabs can cover an entire splashback area with just one or two pieces, virtually eliminating grout lines. The result is a sleek, uninterrupted surface that is incredibly easy to clean and maintain.

These panels are available in a huge range of finishes, including convincing marble replicas, concrete looks, solid colours and even metallic effects. For open-plan kitchens where the splashback is visible from the living area, large format panels deliver a polished, high-end look. The trade-off is that installation requires specialist equipment and expertise, as the panels are heavy, fragile before installation and must be cut with precision.

Glass Splashbacks vs Tile: Which Should You Choose?

Glass splashbacks were the dominant choice in Sydney kitchens throughout the 2010s, and they still offer some genuine advantages. A glass splashback is completely seamless with no grout lines to clean, it can be colour-matched to virtually any shade, and it reflects light to brighten smaller kitchens. However, glass does show fingerprints and cooking splatter more readily than textured tiles, and it cannot be easily repaired if it chips or cracks.

Tiles offer more design versatility, a wider range of textures and the ability to replace individual damaged tiles without redoing the entire splashback. In 2026, the trend is clearly swinging back toward tiles, particularly as large format porcelain panels now offer many of the same seamless benefits that glass once had exclusively. That said, glass remains an excellent choice for rental properties and contemporary kitchens where a clean, uniform colour is the priority.

Colour Trends for 2026: Sage Green, Warm Neutrals and Deep Navy

The colour palette for kitchen splashbacks in 2026 reflects a broader movement toward nature-inspired, calming interiors. Sage green is the standout colour of the year, appearing in everything from gloss subway tiles to matte large format panels. It pairs beautifully with white, timber and stone benchtops and adds a sense of freshness without being overpowering.

Warm neutrals are replacing the cool greys that dominated the last decade. Think creamy whites, soft beiges, warm taupes and mushroom tones. These colours create kitchens that feel inviting and timeless rather than clinical. On the bolder end of the spectrum, deep navy and rich midnight blue splashbacks are gaining popularity in kitchens with lighter cabinetry, creating sophisticated contrast and a sense of depth.

Matching Your Splashback to Your Benchtop

One of the most common questions we hear from homeowners is how to coordinate their splashback with their benchtop. The general principle is contrast: if your benchtop is busy with veining or pattern, opt for a simpler, more restrained splashback. If your benchtop is a solid colour, your splashback is the opportunity to introduce texture and visual interest.

A popular approach in 2026 is to extend the benchtop material up as a short splashback (roughly 100-200mm) and then transition to a different tile above. This creates a cohesive base while still allowing for a feature tile. Another approach gaining traction is the full-height splashback, running tile or stone all the way from benchtop to overhead cabinetry or rangehood, which delivers a dramatic and unified look.

Budget Guide for Splashback Materials

Understanding costs is essential for planning your kitchen splashback project. Here is a general guide for supply and installation in Sydney in 2026:

  • Standard ceramic subway tiles: $80 - $150 per square metre installed
  • Porcelain feature tiles (patterned, terrazzo-look): $120 - $220 per square metre installed
  • Large format porcelain panels: $200 - $400 per square metre installed
  • Natural stone (marble, travertine): $250 - $500+ per square metre installed
  • Glass splashback: $180 - $350 per square metre installed

These prices vary depending on the complexity of the layout, the amount of cutting required, substrate preparation needs and whether waterproofing is necessary. Always obtain a detailed, itemised quote before committing to any work.

Maintenance Considerations

Different splashback materials come with different maintenance requirements, and this is worth factoring into your decision. Glazed ceramic and porcelain tiles are the easiest to maintain, requiring only a wipe-down with a damp cloth and occasional grout cleaning. Natural stone requires periodic sealing, typically every 12 to 24 months, to prevent staining from oil and acidic foods. Glass is easy to clean but shows every mark, so it requires more frequent wiping to maintain its appearance.

Grout maintenance is a consideration with any tiled splashback. Using an epoxy grout rather than standard cement grout adds to the initial cost but dramatically reduces staining and simplifies long-term cleaning, particularly in the high-splash zone directly behind the stove and sink.

Why Professional Installation Matters for Splashbacks

A kitchen splashback might look like a straightforward tiling job, but it is one of the areas where professional installation makes the biggest difference. The wall behind a kitchen bench is rarely perfectly flat or plumb, and any irregularities become immediately visible once tiles are applied. A professional tiler will level and prepare the substrate before installation, ensuring every tile sits flush and every grout line is consistent.

Waterproofing is another critical consideration, particularly around sinks and behind dishwashers where moisture can penetrate behind tiles and damage the wall substrate over time. A qualified tiler will apply appropriate waterproof membranes to these zones, protecting your investment and preventing costly repairs down the track. Precise cutting around power points, window reveals and rangehood outlets also requires experience and specialist tools to achieve a clean finish.
